## 1. Overall Overview

Women's Education Project has built something durable. The mission line — *Her Leadership, Her Livelihood* — is distinctive and human. The Leadership Academy / LAMP model and the Collaboration Lab give the program clear shape. Governance, India-based leadership, financials going back to 2012, annual updates through 2025, and student/alumnae stories are all visible on the public site. These are the assets most nonprofits at this stage are still trying to assemble.

The diagnosis is that the growth question for WEP is not whether the mission story is strong enough. It is whether the **supporter pathways around that story** are reliable, packaged, and easy to act on. From the public review, two soft spots stand out: a footer Get Involved link that returned a 404 during the test crawl, and homepage impact counters that rendered as 0 even though body copy cites 4,000+ alumnae. Both are reversible, but each one quietly costs trust on first visit. The recommended primary focus for the next 30 days is supporter-pathway repair plus donor packaging; broader SEO expansion and a redesign should wait.

The rest of this report walks the fourteen intake categories in the order the intake form asked them, then closes with an overall result and a focus/plan section.

## 6. Current Challenges

### Footer Get Involved link returned 404 during the test crawl

- **Status:** Found on website
- **Evidence / Source URL:** Looked at https://womenseducationproject.org/get-involved/ on 2026-06-25.
- **Finding:** The footer Get Involved link returned a 404 in the test capture, which blocks volunteer/partner/advisor engagement and degrades crawl quality.
- **Recommendation:** Restore, replace, or 301-redirect `/get-involved/` this week. If a full page is not ready yet, ship a one-paragraph holding page ("Here are the ways to help — please email us at [address] and we'll route you to the right person") with an email link while the real page is built. *Why this helps:* a 404 tells a would-be volunteer or partner "we don't know what to do with you." Even a one-paragraph holding page tells them "we want to hear from you — here's where to start." It also stops Google from flagging the URL as broken in search results.
- **Priority:** High

### Homepage impact counters render as 0 while body copy cites 4,000+ alumnae

- **Status:** Found on website
- **Evidence / Source URL:** https://womenseducationproject.org/
- **Finding:** The captured homepage showed counter labels (Alumnae, Careers Started, Enrolled) rendered as 0, even though body copy cites 4,000+ alumnae.
- **Recommendation:** Have whoever maintains the site track down where the counter numbers come from (a CMS field, an animation/JavaScript script that counts up on scroll, or a third-party widget), then either fix the data source or replace the counters with the values WEP wants to display — for example, the 4,000+ alumnae figure already cited elsewhere in body copy. *Why this helps:* a homepage shouting "0 alumnae, 0 careers started, 0 enrolled" while paragraph copy says 4,000+ alumnae forces visitors to decide who to believe. Most won't decide — they'll feel something is off and leave. Matching the numbers to the story restores the credibility WEP has already earned.
- **Priority:** High

### Multiple inbound channels exist without visible follow-up ownership

- **Status:** Found on website
- **Evidence / Source URL:** https://womenseducationproject.org/contact-us/; https://womenseducationproject.org/donate/
- **Finding:** The site exposes donate, contact, founder/general emails, phone, mailing address, forum sponsorship inquiries, and newsletter. Public pages do not show who responds, by when, or in what tone.
- **Recommendation:** Covered in detail in §14. Short version: build a small internal cheat-sheet that says, for each public channel (donate, contact form, founder email, general email, sponsorship inquiry), *who* responds and *by when*, then add one short line — "We'll get back to you within X business days" — on the highest-volume forms. *Why this helps:* unanswered inquiries are the quiet leak in most nonprofit pipelines. A donor who emails and waits two weeks rarely emails again. Telling people what to expect lowers their anxiety *and* gives the team a clear internal target.
- **Priority:** Medium

## 8. Donor & Supporter Readiness

### Donate page is warm and offers monthly giving

- **Status:** Found on website
- **Evidence / Source URL:** https://womenseducationproject.org/donate/
- **Finding:** The donate page uses warm, mission-aligned language ("light the path toward independence, dignity, and a brighter tomorrow") and offers online, mail, and email options. The Circle of Light monthly program is featured.
- **Recommendation:** Don't touch the warmth — "light the path toward independence, dignity, and a brighter tomorrow" is exactly the voice WEP should keep. The gap is *packaging* (what does a gift actually do, in concrete terms?), not *voice*. *Why this helps:* the donate page already does the emotional work. Adding practical detail on top (gift impact, monthly value proposition) lets undecided donors say yes without losing the warmth that made them open the page in the first place.
- **Priority:** Low

### Gift impact tiers and program-cost examples are not concrete

- **Status:** Found in intake
- **Evidence / Source URL:** intake:donor_supporter_readiness.donation_experience; https://womenseducationproject.org/donate/
- **Finding:** A donor who already trusts WEP can give today. A donor who is still deciding is being asked to choose an amount without a concrete picture of what that amount produces.
- **Recommendation:** Once WEP confirms the figures internally, add two short blocks to the donate page: (1) a **gift-impact block** showing example *ranges* — not exact promises — for what student-level, cohort-level, or partner-academy-level gifts typically support; (2) a **Circle of Light value-proposition block** naming monthly tiers (e.g., $25 / $50 / $100 per month) and what they sustain across a year. Use phrases like "helps support" or "contributes toward" rather than "pays for," so no single donor is implicitly promised one specific student's outcome. **Do not publish unverified cost figures.** *Why this helps:* a warm-but-unspecific donate page converts the donors who already trust WEP. A donor who is still deciding needs a concrete picture before they say yes. Ranges (not promises) give them that picture without overstating what any one gift achieves — and the monthly value proposition is what turns one-time donors into Circle of Light supporters, which is predictable revenue WEP can plan against.
- **Priority:** High

### Donor follow-up cadence is not visible publicly

- **Status:** Not found
- **Evidence / Source URL:** Looked at https://womenseducationproject.org/donate/ on 2026-06-25.
- **Finding:** The donate page does not name how donors are thanked, updated, or re-engaged after a gift.
- **Recommendation:** Internally, decide a simple after-the-gift rhythm — for example: thank-you email within 48 hours → quarterly program update → at least one story share per year — then add one short line near the donate button telling donors what to expect ("You'll receive a thank-you within 2 business days and a quarterly update on the students your gift supports."). Confirm timing with the team first; do not over-promise. *Why this helps:* most donors who lapse don't lapse because they stopped caring — they lapse because the relationship went silent after the gift. Telling people what's coming sets expectations *and* gives the WEP team a clear cadence to hold themselves to. One short line on the page that quietly improves donor retention.
- **Priority:** Medium

## 14. Operations & Automation Opportunities

### Inbound-channel ownership is not visible

- **Status:** Found in intake
- **Evidence / Source URL:** intake:current_goals.operations_challenge; https://womenseducationproject.org/contact-us/; https://womenseducationproject.org/donate/; https://womenseducationproject.org/forum-red-bangle/
- **Finding:** Multiple inbound channels (donate, contact form, founder email, general email, sponsorship inquiries, volunteer inquiries, partner inquiries) carry implicit follow-up commitments. Ownership and target response times are not stated.
- **Recommendation:** Build a one-page internal map (a small table is fine) listing each public channel — donate form, contact form, founder email, general email, sponsorship inquiry, volunteer inquiry, partner inquiry — and for each: *who* owns the reply, the *target response time*, and *who covers* if the owner is out. Keep this map internal. On the highest-volume forms (donate, contact, sponsorship), surface one short line telling the submitter when to expect a reply (e.g., "We'll respond within 3 business days."). *Why this helps:* most "we have an operations problem" feelings inside small nonprofits trace back to inquiries falling between people. A simple owner-and-window map removes "I thought you had it" ambiguity, and the public response-window line manages supporter expectations *before* they start refreshing their inbox. It also makes onboarding any new staff or volunteer dramatically faster.
- **Priority:** High

### Automation candidates have clear, named targets

- **Status:** Found in intake
- **Evidence / Source URL:** intake:operations_automation.automation_opportunities
- **Finding:** Candidate automations (donor receipt cadence, Circle of Light welcome sequence, contact-form acknowledgement, sponsorship inquiry routing) all map to existing public channels.
- **Recommendation:** Before automating anything, write down the *manual step* each candidate automation replaces (e.g., "today the founder personally replies to every sponsorship inquiry; we want an acknowledgement auto-reply within 1 hour plus a human follow-up within 3 business days"). Ship the channel-ownership map above first so each automation has a clear definition to follow. Do not introduce a new platform, CRM, or email tool that the team did not ask for. *Why this helps:* automations work when they replace a known manual step. They quietly fail when they're bolted onto an unclear process — because nobody knows what "good" looks like, so the team can't tell whether the automation is helping or hiding problems. Mapping the manual step first means each automation has a measurable bar to clear.
- **Priority:** Medium

## 16. Overall Result & Practical Plan

### Overall result

WEP is not held back by mission, programs, or trust artifacts — those are real strengths and should not be touched in the next 90 days. The actual constraint is **supporter-pathway reliability and packaging**: a broken Get Involved link, homepage counters reading 0, and a donate page with warm voice but no concrete gift impact. Each of these quietly costs trust on a first visit. The good news is that they are small, reversible fixes — and fixing them first compounds the value of every later move (more SEO, more press, more story embeds, more grant applications). Broader SEO expansion, paid acquisition, and a full redesign should wait until the front porch is repaired.

### What to focus on first

These are the highest-leverage repairs and should land inside the next 30 days:

- **Current Challenges (§6) and Website & Digital Presence (§7).** Fix the `/get-involved/` 404 and stabilize the homepage impact counters. Lock the canonical one-sentence definition across the homepage `<title>`, meta description, hero, and social previews.
- **Donor & Supporter Readiness (§8).** Once gift-impact and Circle of Light ranges are confirmed internally, publish a short gift-impact block and a monthly-giving value proposition on /donate/.
- **Volunteer Readiness (§9), starting after §6 is done.** Build out three to five named volunteer pathways on the restored Get Involved page, with one named internal owner per pathway.

### What can wait

These are real but not yet ripe. Picking them up before the items above just creates churn:

- **Programs & Services (§4).** No new program is needed. Tighten the one-sentence program summary (§11) and reuse it everywhere — that does most of the work a new program write-up would do.
- **Grant Readiness (§13).** Once §10 (outcome context note) and §11 (locked program summary) are done, the grant packaging mostly assembles itself. No separate grant content stream is needed in the first 30 days.
- **Operations & Automation (§14), beyond the channel-ownership map.** Ship the ownership map first so each future automation has a defined manual step to replace. Defer any new tool purchases until ownership is decided.
- **SEO expansion, paid acquisition, full redesign.** All three amplify whatever state the site is in. Spend on amplification *after* the visible breakage is repaired.

### Why this order matters

Each step makes the next one cheaper, and each step skipped makes the later steps more expensive:

1. **Fix the broken pathway first** so later traffic doesn't bounce off a 404 or "0 alumnae" counter.
2. **Package gift impact next** so the donors who *are* arriving have a concrete reason to give today instead of "maybe later."
3. **Build out volunteer pathways third**, because by then there is a working page to point them to.
4. **Frame trust and methodology context fourth**, since those edits compound on top of a site that already looks reliable.
5. **Tune SEO and AI-search signals last**, so amplification lands on a site that earns the visit.

Reversing this order — for example, running ads before the Get Involved page is fixed — typically wastes the spend twice: once on the click that bounces, and once on the brand impression that says "this org isn't quite together."
