## 2. What This Area Evaluates

Grant Readiness evaluates whether the nonprofit has clear public materials that could support funder research, conversations, and later grant-writing preparation.

A strong grant-readiness foundation should make these things clear:

- The community need or problem being addressed.
- Who is served.
- What programs or activities are provided.
- What outcomes, outputs, stories, or impact proof are available.
- Why funding is needed and what it supports.
- Whether the organization has basic trust and transparency materials available.
- Whether public language is funder-friendly without being overly technical.

This area does **not** write grant applications, provide legal/accounting advice, determine eligibility, or confirm compliance. It only reviews operational readiness and public evidence quality.

---

## 3. Intake Signals to Use

Relevant intake fields:

```text
- Field/key: grant_readiness_status
  Why it matters: Shows whether grants are active, planned, or not a current priority.
  Strong signal: Clear status such as “we actively apply” or “we want to apply but need stronger outcomes language.”
  Weak or missing signal: Grant interest is vague or not connected to current capacity.

- Field/key: grant_related_materials
  Why it matters: Shows which funder-facing materials may already exist.
  Strong signal: Program descriptions, outcomes, evaluation approach, partner letters, organizational background, or public report are identified.
  Weak or missing signal: “We are not sure” or materials exist only informally.

- Field/key: current_programs_or_services
  Why it matters: Funders need to understand program need, activities, audience, and expected results.
  Strong signal: Programs are described clearly with audience and purpose.
  Weak or missing signal: Programs are named but not explained.

- Field/key: impact_description
  Why it matters: Grant readiness depends on outcomes, outputs, stories, or learning evidence.
  Strong signal: Clear outcome data, basic numbers served, stories, or feedback results.
  Weak or missing signal: Impact is broad, anecdotal, or not yet tracked.

- Field/key: implementation_capacity_30_days
  Why it matters: Keeps recommendations realistic.
  Strong signal: Capacity for public copy edits, a one-page summary, or organizing public proof.
  Weak or missing signal: Capacity unknown.
```

Rules:

- Do not request private grant applications, confidential funder correspondence, private financial statements, or sensitive budget details.
- Do not assume the nonprofit is grant-ready or not grant-ready from public evidence alone.
- Use intake to understand internal materials; use public evidence to assess what an outside funder can easily review.
- Mark private or uncertain materials as `Needs confirmation`.

## 4. Public Evidence to Collect

Primary evidence:

- Program pages.
- About/Mission page.
- Outcomes, impact, annual report, or public impact summary.
- Funder-facing page or language if public.
- Public partner/funder/community proof.
- Public reports or transparency materials.
- Evidence of who is served, what need exists, and what results are tracked.
- Contact or partnership inquiry path.

For this area, collect:

```text
Evidence item: Program need and audience clarity
Where to look: Program pages, homepage, About/Mission page.
What counts as strong evidence: The page explains who is served, what need exists, and why the program matters.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Programs are listed without audience, need, or context.
Suggested citation format: Program page need/audience language, [URL]

Evidence item: Program activities and approach
Where to look: Program pages, service descriptions, impact page.
What counts as strong evidence: Activities are clear enough for a funder to understand what funding supports.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Program language is broad, internal, or unclear.
Suggested citation format: Program approach language, [URL]

Evidence item: Outcomes, outputs, or impact proof
Where to look: Impact page, annual report, program pages, news updates.
What counts as strong evidence: Outcomes, numbers served, stories, feedback, or examples show results or learning.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Impact claims are broad or unsupported.
Suggested citation format: Impact/outcome evidence, [URL]

Evidence item: Organizational credibility and trust materials
Where to look: Leadership, transparency, annual report, partner proof, public profile pages.
What counts as strong evidence: Basic credibility materials are easy to find.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Funder may have to search for leadership, reports, partners, or proof of activity.
Suggested citation format: Trust/transparency evidence, [URL]

Evidence item: Funder/partner next step
Where to look: Contact, partnership, support, or funder pages.
What counts as strong evidence: Funders or partners can identify a clear contact path.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: No obvious way for funders or partners to ask questions.
Suggested citation format: Funder/partner contact path, [URL]
```

Evidence rules:

- Prefer public materials linked from the nonprofit’s own website.
- Do not retrieve, store, or quote private grant documents.
- Do not evaluate eligibility for a specific grant unless a separate human-led process is created.
- If public materials are missing, label them `Not found`; do not assume the organization lacks them internally.

## 5. Strong Signals

Strong Grant Readiness signals:

- Program need, activities, audience, and outcomes are clearly described.
- Public materials show impact, outputs, stories, or evaluation readiness.
- The organization can explain why funding is needed and what it supports.
- Trust and transparency materials are easy to find.
- Public reports or impact summaries are current enough to support funder confidence.
- Partner, funder, or community proof is visible where appropriate.
- Contact or partnership inquiry path is clear.
- Program language is specific enough to reuse in funder conversations.
- The organization distinguishes current proof from future goals.
- Recommendations can be implemented without exposing sensitive financial or client data.

---

## 6. Warning Signs

Grant Readiness warning signs:

- Programs are listed without outcomes or clear audience.
- Impact claims are broad or unsupported.
- No funder-facing summary language is visible.
- Need, activities, outputs, and outcomes are mixed together or unclear.
- Public reports or impact materials are missing, outdated, or buried.
- Trust materials are difficult to find.
- The website explains mission but not why funding is needed.
- Grant interest is stated in intake, but public pages do not support funder due diligence.
- Recommendations would require private financial or client data before a secure workflow exists.

Rules:

- Use supportive language.
- Avoid saying the nonprofit is “not grant-ready” as a final judgment.
- Avoid funder eligibility, legal, financial, or compliance determinations.
- Phrase findings as funder-readiness improvement opportunities.

## 7. Recommended Analysis Pattern

Use this structure:

```text
1. Start with the nonprofit’s stated grant readiness status and materials.
2. Review public program, impact, trust, and contact-path evidence.
3. Identify the main funder-readiness gap: need clarity, program approach, outcomes proof, evaluation language, public trust materials, or funder next step.
4. Explain why the gap matters for funders and partner conversations.
5. Recommend one practical 30-day improvement.
6. Recommend one 90-day grant-readiness improvement if capacity allows.
7. Mark missing or uncertain items as “Needs confirmation.”
```

Do not turn this into grant-writing advice. The goal is to prepare public materials and evidence so a future human grant process has better inputs.

## 8. Common Recommendations

### Recommendation 1: Create a one-page program summary

```text
When to use: Program pages are unclear or too scattered for funder review.
Evidence required: Program page, About page, and intake program review.
30-day version: Create a simple summary with audience, need, activities, and expected outcomes.
90-day version: Build a funder-ready program overview page with outputs, outcomes, stories, and funding needs.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Gives funders a clearer starting point for understanding the work.
Risk if ignored: Funders may have to piece together basic program information from multiple pages.
```

### Recommendation 2: Add outcome or output language

```text
When to use: Program pages explain activities but not results or evidence.
Evidence required: Program page, impact page, annual report, and intake impact review.
30-day version: Add basic outputs or stories, such as people served, services delivered, or recent examples.
90-day version: Develop a simple outcomes/evaluation summary that distinguishes outputs, outcomes, and learning questions.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Helps funders understand what changes because of the program.
Risk if ignored: The program may sound active but not clearly measurable or fundable.
```

### Recommendation 3: Clarify the community need

```text
When to use: The site describes services but not the problem, gap, or community need.
Evidence required: Homepage, About, program page, and public source review if available.
30-day version: Add a short need statement to the relevant program page using plain, non-alarmist language.
90-day version: Build a stronger needs-and-context section with public data sources or community evidence if appropriate.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Helps funders understand why the program matters now.
Risk if ignored: Funding requests may feel less urgent or less connected to community need.
```

### Recommendation 4: Organize public trust materials for funders

```text
When to use: Leadership, impact, reports, or partner proof exist but are scattered or hard to find.
Evidence required: Trust/transparency, About, impact, report, and partner proof review.
30-day version: Add a simple “For Funders / Our Impact & Accountability” link group from the About or Impact page.
90-day version: Create a funder-facing readiness page with program summary, impact proof, leadership, reports, and contact path.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Makes due diligence easier for funders and partners.
Risk if ignored: Useful credibility materials may remain hidden.
```

### Recommendation 5: Add a clear funder or partnership contact path

```text
When to use: Interested funders or partners have no clear way to ask questions.
Evidence required: Contact, partnership, About, and footer review.
30-day version: Add a short funder/partner inquiry note to the contact page with the appropriate public contact path.
90-day version: Create a lightweight partnership/funder inquiry process that routes requests to the right person.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Reduces friction for funders and partners who want to learn more.
Risk if ignored: Interested funders or partners may not know who to contact.
```

### Recommendation 6: Distinguish current evidence from future goals

```text
When to use: The website mixes aspirations, activities, and outcomes in a way that may confuse funders.
Evidence required: Program, impact, annual report, and campaign language review.
30-day version: Edit copy to separate current activities, known results, and future goals.
90-day version: Build a measurement narrative that explains what is tracked now and what the team wants to learn next.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Makes the organization’s evidence more credible and easier to review.
Risk if ignored: Funders may be unsure which claims are proven results versus goals.
```

## 9. What NOT to Say

Do not say:

- “You are not grant-ready.”
- “Funders will reject this.”
- “You qualify/do not qualify for grants.”
- “This will help you win grants.”
- “Your budget is insufficient.”
- “Your evaluation plan is compliant/noncompliant.”
- “You should submit this to a funder as-is.”
- “You must publish private financial or program data.”

Safer alternatives:

- “These materials could be stronger for funder review.”
- “A clearer program summary may make funder conversations easier.”
- “This information should be confirmed before it is reused in a grant application.”
- “Any legal, financial, or compliance-sensitive materials should be reviewed by the appropriate professional.”
- “This is an operational readiness observation, not a grant eligibility determination.”

---

## 10. Safety, Privacy, and Disclaimer Rules

Use these rules:

- Do not request private grant applications, private budgets, donor lists, client/member data, employee records, confidential funder correspondence, bank information, passwords, or private financial statements.
- Do not provide legal, tax, accounting, grant eligibility, or compliance advice.
- Do not promise grant funding or funder response outcomes.
- Do not evaluate a specific grant opportunity unless a separate human-led process is built.
- Distinguish public evidence from intake answers and assumptions.
- Label uncertain items as `Needs confirmation`.
- Draft for human review before customer delivery.

If Grant Readiness overlaps with **Financial & Compliance Readiness**, the related client-facing section must include:

> This section is an operational readiness review, not accounting, tax, or legal advice.

## 12. Report Output Guidance

Recommended client-facing section format:

```text
### Grant Readiness

What we saw:
- Evidence-based observation from program pages, impact materials, trust materials, funder-facing language, or contact path.

Why it matters:
- Plain-language explanation tied to funder understanding, due diligence, program credibility, or future grant preparation.

Recommended next step:
- One practical action that matches the nonprofit’s stated grant priority and 30-day capacity.

30-day action:
- Specific program summary, impact language, need statement, trust-material, or contact-path improvement.

90-day opportunity:
- Larger funder-facing page, outcome summary, or readiness package if the team has capacity.

Evidence used:
- Source URL or intake field.

Needs confirmation:
- Any unclear or unverified item.
```

Tone rules:

- Warm, practical, and respectful.
- Avoid sounding like a grant reviewer rejecting the organization.
- Focus on readiness and public evidence quality.
- Do not overstate certainty.
- Keep financial/compliance-sensitive items for professional review.

## 13. Example Output Snippets

### Strong Example

```text
What we saw: The program page explains the audience served, the core activities, and includes recent outcome examples.

Why it matters: This gives funders a clearer picture of what funding supports and what type of change the program is working toward.

Recommended next step: Add one short section that distinguishes current outputs from longer-term outcomes so the evidence is easier to reuse in future funder materials.
```

### Missing Evidence Example

```text
What we saw: We could not find public outcome, output, or impact language connected to the main program pages.

Why it matters: Funders often need to understand not only what a program does, but what it produces, improves, or helps the community learn.

Recommended next step: Add a simple impact block with one story, one output metric if available, and one outcome the program is working toward.
```

### Needs Confirmation Example

```text
What we saw: The intake notes mention grant-related materials, but we could not confirm a public funder-facing summary or impact report on the website.

Why it matters: If these materials are intended to support funder conversations, making a public summary easier to find could strengthen readiness.

Recommended next step: Confirm which materials are safe to make public, then create a short funder-facing summary page or link group.
```
