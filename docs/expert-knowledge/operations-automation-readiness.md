## 2. What This Area Evaluates

Operations & Automation Readiness evaluates whether the nonprofit has public-facing information and internal readiness signals that help outside audiences understand the work and take the next step.

A strong foundation should make these things clear:

- Team can identify repeatable workflows.
- Public communication/reporting needs are clear.
- There are safe, non-sensitive automation opportunities.
- Existing tools can support a simple workflow.
- Workflow inputs and outputs are clear.
- A 30-day improvement can be done without new complex software.

This area is an operational readiness review only. It should help connect intake answers, public evidence, expert standards, and practical next steps without overclaiming.

## 3. Intake Signals to Use

Relevant intake fields:

```text
- Field/key: manual_time_tasks
  Why it matters: Shows which tasks take too much manual time.
  Strong signal: Specific repeatable workflows are identified.
  Weak or missing signal: Team is overwhelmed but cannot name priority workflows.

- Field/key: current_tools_or_workflows
  Why it matters: Automation recommendations should fit tools the team already uses.
  Strong signal: Tools like Google Workspace, Microsoft 365, CRM, CMS, spreadsheets, or project management system are identified.
  Weak or missing signal: Tools are unknown or disconnected.

- Field/key: implementation_capacity_30_days
  Why it matters: Keeps recommendations realistic.
  Strong signal: Capacity for a small template, form, checklist, or routing change.
  Weak or missing signal: Capacity unknown.

- Field/key: reporting_or_content_cadence
  Why it matters: Public updates and reports can reveal repeatable content workflows.
  Strong signal: Regular newsletters, board reports, updates, or program reports are needed.
  Weak or missing signal: No cadence or owner is clear.

- Field/key: data_sensitivity_boundary
  Why it matters: Safe automation requires knowing what data should not be handled.
  Strong signal: Workflow can use public or non-sensitive data.
  Weak or missing signal: Workflow would require donor/client/private financial data.
```

Rules:

- Do not treat intake answers as proven public facts.
- Use intake to understand goals, constraints, and stated concerns.
- Confirm outward-facing claims with public evidence when possible.
- If a signal is missing or private, mark it as `Needs confirmation`.

## 4. Public Evidence to Collect

Primary evidence:

- Public repeated content needs.
- Manual process pain points.
- Existing tool context.
- Safe non-sensitive automation opportunity.
- Reporting or follow-up gap.

For this area, collect:

```text
Evidence item: Public repeated content needs
Where to look: News, blog, events, newsletters, reports, social links.
What counts as strong evidence: Recurring communication/reporting pattern is visible.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Content need exists but public cadence is unclear.
Suggested citation format: Public update/reporting cadence, [URL]

Evidence item: Manual process pain points
Where to look: Intake answers and admin notes.
What counts as strong evidence: Specific task, owner, input, output, and frequency can be described.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Pain point is broad or undefined.
Suggested citation format: Intake manual process signal

Evidence item: Existing tool context
Where to look: Intake answers only unless public.
What counts as strong evidence: Workflow can be built around existing tools.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Tool stack unknown.
Suggested citation format: Intake tool/workflow signal

Evidence item: Safe non-sensitive automation opportunity
Where to look: Intake + public workflow evidence.
What counts as strong evidence: Task uses public/non-sensitive info or admin-approved summary data.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Task requires sensitive donor/client/financial data.
Suggested citation format: Automation safety review

Evidence item: Reporting or follow-up gap
Where to look: Volunteer, donor, grant, program, and content workflows.
What counts as strong evidence: A template/checklist/routing step can reduce friction.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Ownership and next step unclear.
Suggested citation format: Workflow gap evidence
```

Evidence rules:

- Prefer public materials linked from the nonprofit's own website.
- Do not request or store sensitive/private documents.
- If evidence is not visible publicly, label it `Not found` or `Needs confirmation`.
- Do not infer private facts from public silence.

## 5. Strong Signals

- Team can identify repeatable workflows.
- Public communication/reporting needs are clear.
- There are safe, non-sensitive automation opportunities.
- Existing tools can support a simple workflow.
- Workflow inputs and outputs are clear.
- A 30-day improvement can be done without new complex software.
- Automation supports staff review rather than replacing judgment.
- Sensitive data boundaries are respected.

---

## 6. Warning Signs

- Team is overwhelmed but cannot identify priority workflows.
- Repetitive tasks are blocking communication or reporting.
- Data/process ownership is unclear.
- Automation idea would require sensitive data before a secure workflow exists.
- The team has many tools but no clear process.
- Manual follow-up creates missed donor, volunteer, or reporting opportunities.
- Recommendations jump to tools before clarifying workflow.

Rules:

- Use supportive language.
- Avoid blame or audit-like phrasing.
- Avoid legal, tax, accounting, HR, or compliance determinations.
- Phrase findings as readiness improvement opportunities.

## 7. Recommended Analysis Pattern

Use this structure:

```text
1. Start with the nonprofit's stated concern or goal.
2. Compare intake signals with public evidence.
3. Identify the main readiness gap.
4. Explain why the gap matters for donors, volunteers, funders, partners, staff, or community members.
5. Recommend one practical 30-day improvement.
6. Recommend one 90-day improvement if capacity allows.
7. Mark uncertain items as “Needs confirmation.”
```

## 8. Common Recommendations

### Recommendation 1: Map one repeatable workflow before automating

```text
When to use: A task feels manual but inputs/outputs are unclear.
Evidence required: Intake workflow pain point.
30-day version: Document trigger, owner, input, output, and review step for one workflow.
90-day version: Turn the mapped workflow into a reusable template or light automation.
Effort level: Low
Expected benefit: Prevents automating a confusing process.
Risk if ignored: Automation may add complexity instead of reducing work.
```

### Recommendation 2: Create a reusable template

```text
When to use: The team repeatedly writes similar emails, reports, updates, or summaries.
Evidence required: Intake + public content/reporting cadence.
30-day version: Create one approved template for the most repeated task.
90-day version: Build a small template library with review rules.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Saves time while keeping human review.
Risk if ignored: Staff keep recreating the same content manually.
```

### Recommendation 3: Add a simple intake or routing form

```text
When to use: Requests or follow-ups arrive through scattered emails.
Evidence required: Intake process pain point.
30-day version: Create a short form or routing checklist for non-sensitive information.
90-day version: Connect routing to a shared tracker or CRM if appropriate.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Reduces missed follow-up and clarifies ownership.
Risk if ignored: Requests may be delayed or lost.
```

### Recommendation 4: Use a tracker for follow-up status

```text
When to use: Volunteer, donor, grant, or program follow-up is hard to monitor.
Evidence required: Intake workflow + existing tools.
30-day version: Create a simple spreadsheet/status tracker with owner and next step.
90-day version: Add reminders or light automation after the process is stable.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Improves visibility without new complex software.
Risk if ignored: Staff may not know what is pending.
```

### Recommendation 5: Keep sensitive workflows human-reviewed

```text
When to use: Automation touches donor, client, financial, HR, or compliance-sensitive areas.
Evidence required: Workflow sensitivity review.
30-day version: Limit automation to drafts, reminders, or summaries for staff review.
90-day version: Design a secure workflow only after data rules and permissions are clear.
Effort level: Low to High
Expected benefit: Reduces risk while still saving time.
Risk if ignored: Unsafe automation could expose sensitive information.
```

## 9. What NOT to Say

Do not say:

- “You should automate everything.”
- “AI can replace this staff work.”
- “Connect all donor/client/financial data now.”
- “This tool will solve the process.”
- “No human review is needed.”
- “This will save X hours per week.”

Safer alternatives:

- “Start by mapping one repeatable workflow.”
- “Use automation for drafts, routing, or reminders with human review.”
- “Keep sensitive data out until a secure workflow is built.”

---

## 10. Safety, Privacy, and Disclaimer Rules

Use these rules:

- Do not request passwords, donor lists, employee records, private client/member data, bank information, private financial statements, confidential grant files, or sensitive internal documents.
- Do not provide legal, tax, accounting, HR, insurance, grant eligibility, or compliance advice.
- Do not promise fundraising, ranking, grant, volunteer, or operational outcomes.
- Distinguish public evidence from intake answers and assumptions.
- Label uncertain items as `Needs confirmation`.
- Draft for human review before customer delivery.
- Keep recommendations operational and public-facing unless a later secure workflow is explicitly built.

If this area overlaps with **Financial & Compliance Readiness**, the related client-facing section must include:

> This section is an operational readiness review, not accounting, tax, or legal advice.
