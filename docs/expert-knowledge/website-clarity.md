## 2. What This Area Evaluates

Website Clarity evaluates whether a busy visitor can quickly understand the nonprofit and take the right next step.

A strong nonprofit website should make these things clear:

- Who the organization serves.
- What problem or need the organization addresses.
- What programs, services, or activities the organization provides.
- Why the work matters.
- What the visitor should do next.
- How to contact, donate, volunteer, partner, or learn more.

This area commonly reveals problems such as vague homepage headlines, confusing navigation, buried contact paths, too many competing calls to action, or pages that describe the organization internally but do not guide an outside visitor.

## 3. Intake Signals to Use

Relevant intake fields:

```text
- Field/key: website_challenge
  Why it matters: Shows what the nonprofit already knows feels difficult about the website.
  Strong signal: Specific issue such as “important actions are hard to find” or “it does not clearly explain what we do.”
  Weak or missing signal: “Outdated” only, without explaining what blocks visitors.

- Field/key: primary_goal
  Why it matters: Website recommendations should support the organization’s current priority.
  Strong signal: Clear priority such as donor action, volunteer signup, grant readiness, or program clarity.
  Weak or missing signal: Broad goal like “improve everything.”

- Field/key: stuck_points
  Why it matters: Connects website clarity to a practical business or mission problem.
  Strong signal: Visitor behavior issue, such as “people visit but do not take action.”
  Weak or missing signal: No connection between the website and desired outcome.

- Field/key: implementation_capacity_30_days
  Why it matters: Keeps recommendations realistic.
  Strong signal: Clear capacity level such as “small copy edits only” or “a few website/page updates.”
  Weak or missing signal: Capacity unknown.

- Field/key: current_programs_or_services
  Why it matters: Helps check whether the website accurately reflects the current work.
  Strong signal: Programs are named clearly and match public pages.
  Weak or missing signal: Intake describes current work that is missing or hard to find online.
```

Rules:

- Do not treat intake answers as proven public facts.
- Use intake to understand the nonprofit’s own pain points and goals.
- Confirm outward-facing claims with the public website when possible.
- If a website issue is only mentioned in intake but not observable, mark it as `Needs confirmation`.

## 4. Public Evidence to Collect

Primary evidence:

- Homepage.
- Main navigation.
- Mobile view or responsive behavior if observable.
- About/Mission page.
- Programs/Services pages.
- Contact page or contact path.
- Primary call to action.
- Footer links.
- Donation, volunteer, or request-help path if relevant.

For this area, collect:

```text
Evidence item: Homepage headline and first screen
Where to look: Homepage hero area above or near the first scroll.
What counts as strong evidence: It says who is served, what the organization does, and gives a clear next step.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Vague mission language, internal slogans, or no clear audience/action.
Suggested citation format: Homepage, hero section, [URL]

Evidence item: Main navigation clarity
Where to look: Top nav and footer nav.
What counts as strong evidence: Important pages are easy to find with plain labels.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Critical pages are buried, hidden, unclear, or named with internal jargon.
Suggested citation format: Main navigation, [URL]

Evidence item: Primary call to action
Where to look: Homepage, header, footer, relevant landing pages.
What counts as strong evidence: One or two clear actions match the nonprofit’s goals.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: No obvious next step or too many competing actions.
Suggested citation format: Homepage CTA, [URL]

Evidence item: Contact path
Where to look: Header, footer, contact page, forms.
What counts as strong evidence: A visitor can quickly find how to reach the organization.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Contact path is missing, buried, broken, or unclear.
Suggested citation format: Contact page/path, [URL]

Evidence item: Program clarity
Where to look: Program, services, impact, or work pages.
What counts as strong evidence: Programs explain who they help, what happens, and how to participate or learn more.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Program names are listed without plain-language explanation.
Suggested citation format: Program page, [URL]
```

Evidence rules:

- Prefer the nonprofit’s own public website first.
- Do not use private documents or credentials.
- Do not infer analytics, traffic, conversion rates, or user behavior unless provided.
- If evidence is not visible publicly, label it `Not found` rather than assuming it does not exist internally.

## 5. Strong Signals

Strong Website Clarity signals:

- A first-time visitor can understand the organization within a few seconds.
- The homepage headline is specific, plain, and audience-focused.
- The site explains who is served and what problem is addressed.
- The primary next step is visible near the top of the homepage.
- Navigation labels are simple and predictable.
- Contact information or contact path is easy to find.
- Programs are explained in plain language, not only by internal program names.
- The website reflects current programs and priorities.
- Mobile visitors can still understand the main message and find the next step.
- The page structure supports donor, volunteer, funder, or community visitor needs.

---

## 6. Warning Signs

Website Clarity warning signs:

- The homepage headline is vague, abstract, or slogan-only.
- The site does not clearly say who the organization serves.
- Important actions are hard to find.
- Multiple competing CTAs appear with no clear priority.
- Program pages use internal names without explaining what they mean.
- The About page describes history but not current work clearly.
- The contact path is missing, buried, or confusing.
- The website appears outdated compared with the intake description.
- Mobile layout makes the main message or CTA hard to use.
- Visitors must already know the organization to understand the site.

Rules:

- Use supportive language.
- Avoid blaming the nonprofit or web vendor.
- Phrase issues as improvement opportunities.
- Do not claim the website is “bad” or “failing.”

---

## 7. Recommended Analysis Pattern

Use this structure:

```text
1. Start with the nonprofit’s stated website concern or goal.
2. Compare that concern with the homepage, navigation, contact path, and primary CTA.
3. Identify the main clarity gap.
4. Explain why the gap matters for donors, volunteers, funders, community members, or partners.
5. Recommend one practical 30-day improvement.
6. Recommend one 90-day improvement if the team has capacity.
7. Mark missing or uncertain items as “Needs confirmation.”
```

Do not give a generic website redesign recommendation unless the evidence clearly supports it. Prefer specific improvements such as headline edits, clearer CTA placement, better navigation labels, or a simple program summary block.

## 8. Common Recommendations

### Recommendation 1: Rewrite the homepage headline for clarity

```text
When to use: The homepage headline is vague, slogan-based, or does not explain who is served.
Evidence required: Homepage hero review.
30-day version: Replace or supplement the headline with a plain-language sentence that says who the nonprofit helps and what it helps them do.
90-day version: Test the updated message across homepage, About page, and key program pages for consistency.
Effort level: Low
Expected benefit: Helps new visitors understand the organization faster.
Risk if ignored: Visitors may care about the mission but leave before understanding the work.
```

### Recommendation 2: Clarify the primary call to action

```text
When to use: The page has no clear next step or too many competing actions.
Evidence required: Homepage CTA review, header/footer review, intake priority.
30-day version: Choose one primary CTA that matches the current goal, such as Donate, Volunteer, Get Help, Partner, or Contact Us.
90-day version: Build a simple visitor pathway from homepage to the action page with supporting context and trust signals.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Makes it easier for motivated visitors to act.
Risk if ignored: Interested visitors may not know what to do next.
```

### Recommendation 3: Simplify navigation labels

```text
When to use: Important pages are hidden, hard to understand, or named with internal language.
Evidence required: Main navigation and footer review.
30-day version: Rename key navigation items with plain labels such as About, Programs, Donate, Volunteer, Contact, or Impact.
90-day version: Reorganize the site around visitor goals instead of internal departments or historical structure.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Helps visitors find important information with less effort.
Risk if ignored: Visitors may miss services, donation paths, volunteer paths, or trust information.
```

### Recommendation 4: Add a clear “Who we serve / What we do” section

```text
When to use: The site names programs but does not explain the audience, problem, or service clearly.
Evidence required: Homepage, About page, and program page review.
30-day version: Add a short section with 2–4 plain-language bullets explaining who is served and what support is provided.
90-day version: Create a concise program overview page that connects programs to outcomes and next steps.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Improves understanding for donors, volunteers, funders, and community members.
Risk if ignored: The organization’s work may seem less concrete than it really is.
```

### Recommendation 5: Make the contact path easier to find

```text
When to use: Contact information or the inquiry path is buried, unclear, or missing.
Evidence required: Header/footer/contact page review.
30-day version: Add a visible Contact link in the header or footer and make sure the page explains what kind of inquiries are welcome.
90-day version: Create simple inquiry paths for different audiences, such as donors, volunteers, partners, and people seeking services.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Reduces friction for people who want to reach the organization.
Risk if ignored: Visitors may give up or contact the wrong channel.
```

### Recommendation 6: Align website content with current programs

```text
When to use: Intake describes current programs or priorities that are missing or unclear on the public website.
Evidence required: Intake program description + public program page review.
30-day version: Add a short update or program summary so public pages reflect current work.
90-day version: Refresh program pages with audiences served, outcomes, eligibility, and next steps.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Makes public messaging more accurate and useful.
Risk if ignored: Funders, partners, donors, or participants may see outdated information.
```

## 9. What NOT to Say

Do not say:

- “Your website is bad.”
- “Your website is failing.”
- “This is why people are not donating.”
- “This will increase donations/conversions by X%.”
- “You need a full redesign” unless the evidence strongly supports that and a human reviewer agrees.
- “Your team is not maintaining the website.”
- “Google will penalize this” unless verified by a specific technical SEO finding.

Safer alternatives:

- “The current homepage could make the organization’s work clearer for first-time visitors.”
- “A clearer next step may help interested visitors take action.”
- “This appears to be a practical improvement opportunity, not a criticism of the organization’s work.”
- “If this information exists elsewhere, it may be worth making it easier to find from the homepage.”
- “This item should be confirmed with the team before finalizing the recommendation.”

## 10. Safety, Privacy, and Disclaimer Rules

Use these rules:

- Do not request passwords, CMS access, analytics access, donor data, private client/member data, or confidential documents.
- Do not infer traffic, conversion rates, donor behavior, or user behavior from the website alone.
- Do not provide legal, tax, accounting, or compliance advice.
- Distinguish public evidence from intake answers and assumptions.
- Label uncertain items as `Needs confirmation`.
- Draft for human review before customer delivery.

This area usually does not require the financial disclaimer. If Website Clarity comments overlap with **Financial & Compliance Readiness**, that section must include:

> This section is an operational readiness review, not accounting, tax, or legal advice.

## 12. Report Output Guidance

Recommended client-facing section format:

```text
### Website Clarity

What we saw:
- Evidence-based observation from the homepage, navigation, CTA, contact path, or program pages.

Why it matters:
- Plain-language explanation tied to donor, volunteer, funder, partner, community, or service-user understanding.

Recommended next step:
- One practical action that matches the nonprofit’s stated priority and 30-day capacity.

30-day action:
- Specific copy, navigation, CTA, or contact-path improvement.

90-day opportunity:
- Larger site organization or content improvement if the team has capacity.

Evidence used:
- Source URL or intake field.

Needs confirmation:
- Any unclear or unverified item.
```

Tone rules:

- Warm, practical, and respectful.
- Avoid sounding like an audit.
- Focus on visitor clarity, not design taste.
- Prefer small, useful improvements before recommending a redesign.
- Do not overstate certainty.

## 13. Example Output Snippets

### Strong Example

```text
What we saw: The homepage quickly explains who the organization serves and includes a visible “Volunteer” action near the top of the page.

Why it matters: This makes it easier for a motivated visitor to understand the mission and take the next step without searching through multiple pages.

Recommended next step: Add one short example of what a volunteer helps make possible so the action feels more concrete.
```

### Missing Evidence Example

```text
What we saw: We could not find a clear contact path from the homepage or main navigation.

Why it matters: If visitors, partners, or potential supporters want to ask a question, they may leave the site before reaching the right person.

Recommended next step: Add a visible Contact link in the header or footer and clarify what types of inquiries should use that path.
```

### Needs Confirmation Example

```text
What we saw: The intake notes say the organization recently changed its program focus, but the public program pages appear to describe the older structure.

Why it matters: When public pages do not reflect current work, donors, funders, volunteers, or participants may misunderstand what the organization currently offers.

Recommended next step: Confirm which program descriptions are current, then update the homepage and program overview with the latest language.
```
