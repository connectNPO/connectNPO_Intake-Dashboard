## 2. What This Area Evaluates

Trust & Transparency evaluates whether the nonprofit provides public credibility signals that help outside audiences understand who leads the organization, what impact it creates, and where to find basic accountability information.

A strong trust and transparency foundation should make these things clear:

- Who leads or governs the organization.
- What mission, programs, and communities the organization serves.
- What impact or outcomes are publicly described.
- Whether recent updates, news, or proof of activity are visible.
- Whether partner, funder, testimonial, or community proof is available.
- Whether public transparency materials are easy to find when appropriate.

This area does not provide legal, accounting, tax, or compliance determinations. It only reviews public operational readiness and trust-building signals.

## 3. Intake Signals to Use

Relevant intake fields:

```text
- Field/key: public_trust_materials
  Why it matters: Shows what the nonprofit believes is already public.
  Strong signal: Board/leadership page, annual report, impact summary, public profile, partner proof, or recent updates are identified.
  Weak or missing signal: “We are not sure” or “None are public yet.”

- Field/key: primary_goal
  Why it matters: Trust signals support donor, volunteer, grant, and partner readiness differently.
  Strong signal: Clear goal around donor confidence, grant readiness, partner outreach, or public credibility.
  Weak or missing signal: No clear connection between trust materials and current priorities.

- Field/key: grant_readiness_materials
  Why it matters: Public reports, outcomes, and partner proof can also support funder readiness.
  Strong signal: Public annual/impact report, outcomes, or organizational background.
  Weak or missing signal: Grant materials exist internally but are not public or are unclear.

- Field/key: donor_challenge
  Why it matters: Donor confidence often depends on impact and accountability signals near the giving path.
  Strong signal: Donor concern mentions trust, proof, impact, or donation page clarity.
  Weak or missing signal: Donor issue is broad without evidence of trust friction.

- Field/key: implementation_capacity_30_days
  Why it matters: Keeps recommendations realistic.
  Strong signal: Capacity for small updates, adding links, publishing summaries, or refreshing pages.
  Weak or missing signal: Capacity unknown.
```

Rules:

- Do not ask for private financial statements, donor lists, board private contact details, or confidential documents.
- Do not treat “not found publicly” as proof that the organization lacks the material internally.
- Use intake to understand what exists and public evidence to verify what a visitor can actually find.
- Mark private or uncertain materials as `Needs confirmation`.

## 4. Public Evidence to Collect

Primary evidence:

- About page.
- Leadership, board, staff, or governance page.
- Annual report, impact report, outcomes summary, or public impact page.
- Public nonprofit profile link if provided or clearly linked.
- Public Form 990 link only if already public and relevant.
- Partner, funder, testimonial, accreditation, or community proof.
- Recent news, updates, blog, newsletter archive, or events.
- Contact page.
- Donation/support page trust signals.

For this area, collect:

```text
Evidence item: Leadership or governance visibility
Where to look: About, Team, Board, Leadership, Governance pages.
What counts as strong evidence: Names, roles, or governance information are visible enough to show accountability.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: No leadership/governance information is public or it is hard to find.
Suggested citation format: Leadership/board page, [URL]

Evidence item: Impact or outcomes proof
Where to look: Impact page, annual report, program pages, news updates.
What counts as strong evidence: Recent outcomes, stories, statistics, or examples show what changed because of the work.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Mission claims are broad but not supported by examples or public outcomes.
Suggested citation format: Impact/outcomes page, [URL]

Evidence item: Public transparency materials
Where to look: Annual report, financial summary, nonprofit profile, public Form 990 link, transparency page.
What counts as strong evidence: Basic transparency materials are easy to find and current enough for public trust.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Materials are missing, outdated, or buried.
Suggested citation format: Transparency material, [URL]

Evidence item: Partner or community proof
Where to look: Partner logos, testimonials, funder lists, community stories, news mentions.
What counts as strong evidence: Relevant third-party or community signals support credibility.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: No public proof beyond the organization’s own claims.
Suggested citation format: Partner/community proof, [URL]

Evidence item: Recent activity signals
Where to look: News, blog, events, social links, newsletter archive, homepage updates.
What counts as strong evidence: Public site shows recent activity or current programs.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Site appears inactive or outdated compared with intake.
Suggested citation format: Recent update/news, [URL]
```

Evidence rules:

- Prefer public materials linked from the nonprofit’s own website.
- Do not retrieve or store sensitive documents.
- Do not judge compliance status from public materials.
- If a public Form 990/profile link is mentioned, frame it as a transparency signal, not tax advice.
- If materials are not public, label them `Not found` or `Needs confirmation`.

## 5. Strong Signals

Strong Trust & Transparency signals:

- Leadership, board, or governance information is visible.
- Mission, programs, and communities served are clear.
- Impact information is current and easy to find.
- Public reports or summaries are available when appropriate.
- Partner, funder, testimonial, or community proof supports credibility.
- Recent news or updates show the organization is active.
- Donation or support pages include nearby trust signals.
- Contact information is easy to find.
- Public accountability materials are written in accessible language.
- Trust materials connect to donor, funder, volunteer, and partner needs.

---

## 6. Warning Signs

Trust & Transparency warning signs:

- No leadership, board, or governance information is visible.
- Impact proof is missing, vague, or outdated.
- Public transparency materials are hard to find.
- Donation asks appear without nearby credibility or impact proof.
- Partner or community proof is missing even when relationships are mentioned.
- Public pages appear inactive or not recently updated.
- The organization claims outcomes but does not provide examples or evidence.
- Important credibility materials exist but are buried in PDFs or old pages.
- Contact information is unclear.

Rules:

- Use supportive language.
- Avoid implying wrongdoing or noncompliance.
- Avoid legal/accounting/tax conclusions.
- Phrase findings as public trust-building opportunities.

## 7. Recommended Analysis Pattern

Use this structure:

```text
1. Start with the nonprofit’s stated trust materials or readiness goal.
2. Review public leadership/governance, impact, transparency, partner proof, and recent activity signals.
3. Identify the main trust gap: visibility, currency, impact proof, public reports, or credibility near the ask.
4. Explain why the gap matters for donors, funders, volunteers, partners, or community members.
5. Recommend one practical 30-day improvement.
6. Recommend one 90-day trust-building improvement if capacity allows.
7. Mark missing or uncertain items as “Needs confirmation.”
```

Do not recommend publishing sensitive documents. Prefer public summaries, links to already-public materials, and plain-language impact/accountability pages.

## 8. Common Recommendations

### Recommendation 1: Make leadership or governance information easier to find

```text
When to use: Leadership, board, staff, or governance information is missing or buried.
Evidence required: About, Team, Board, Leadership, and footer review.
30-day version: Add or link a simple leadership/board page with names and roles appropriate for public display.
90-day version: Build a stronger About/Leadership section that explains governance, team responsibilities, and accountability.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Helps visitors understand who is responsible for the organization.
Risk if ignored: Donors, partners, or funders may have to search for basic accountability information.
```

### Recommendation 2: Add or refresh impact proof

```text
When to use: The site states mission or programs but gives little evidence of outcomes or activity.
Evidence required: Impact, program, annual report, and news/update review.
30-day version: Add 2–3 recent impact examples, outcomes, or stories to the homepage or impact page.
90-day version: Create a simple impact summary page that connects programs, outcomes, stories, and next steps.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Helps visitors see that the organization’s work is active and meaningful.
Risk if ignored: The organization’s public credibility may depend too heavily on broad mission statements.
```

### Recommendation 3: Create a simple transparency hub

```text
When to use: Reports, public profiles, leadership, and impact information exist but are scattered.
Evidence required: About, impact, annual report, nonprofit profile, and footer review.
30-day version: Add a “Transparency” or “Our Impact & Accountability” section that links to public materials.
90-day version: Build a dedicated page with impact summary, public reports, leadership, partner proof, and contact path.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Makes trust materials easier for donors, funders, and partners to review.
Risk if ignored: Useful trust materials may exist but remain hard to find.
```

### Recommendation 4: Place trust signals near donation or support asks

```text
When to use: Donate/support page asks for action without nearby credibility or impact proof.
Evidence required: Donation/support page, impact page, and transparency material review.
30-day version: Add a short impact/trust block near the donation ask with links to public impact or accountability information.
90-day version: Build a donor trust section that combines gift impact, transparency, recent outcomes, and contact information.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Helps interested supporters feel more confident before taking action.
Risk if ignored: Visitors may need to leave the giving path to find credibility information.
```

### Recommendation 5: Add recent activity or update signals

```text
When to use: The site appears inactive, outdated, or does not show recent work.
Evidence required: News, blog, events, homepage, program page, and social/update review.
30-day version: Add a recent update, program note, or impact highlight to show current activity.
90-day version: Create a simple quarterly update rhythm for public news, impact notes, or community stories.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Shows the organization is active and current.
Risk if ignored: Visitors may wonder whether the information is still accurate.
```

### Recommendation 6: Add partner or community proof

```text
When to use: The nonprofit mentions partnerships, community trust, or funder relationships but does not show public proof.
Evidence required: Partner, testimonial, funder, event, and community story review.
30-day version: Add public partner names/logos or short community proof only where permission is clear.
90-day version: Build a partner/community proof section with quotes, stories, and links to relevant public sources.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Adds third-party context that can strengthen credibility.
Risk if ignored: Trust may rely only on the organization’s own claims.
```

## 9. What NOT to Say

Do not say:

- “Your organization is not transparent.”
- “You are not compliant.”
- “Donors will not trust you.”
- “Your board information is legally insufficient.”
- “Your Form 990 is missing/incorrect.”
- “You should publish private financial statements.”
- “This proves your organization is inactive.”
- “You must disclose this information.”

Safer alternatives:

- “Public trust materials could be easier for visitors to find.”
- “Adding a plain-language impact summary may help donors and partners understand the work more quickly.”
- “If this material exists internally, it may be worth creating a public summary or link.”
- “Any compliance, legal, tax, or accounting questions should be reviewed by the appropriate professional.”
- “This is an operational readiness observation, not a compliance determination.”

---

## 10. Safety, Privacy, and Disclaimer Rules

Use these rules:

- Do not request private financial statements, donor lists, board private contact details, employee records, client/member data, confidential grant files, passwords, or internal governance documents.
- Do not provide legal, tax, accounting, governance, charitable solicitation, or compliance advice.
- Do not make compliance determinations.
- Do not tell the nonprofit to publish sensitive documents.
- Distinguish public evidence from intake answers and assumptions.
- Label uncertain items as `Needs confirmation`.
- Draft for human review before customer delivery.

If Trust & Transparency overlaps with **Financial & Compliance Readiness**, the related client-facing section must include:

> This section is an operational readiness review, not accounting, tax, or legal advice.

## 12. Report Output Guidance

Recommended client-facing section format:

```text
### Trust & Transparency

What we saw:
- Evidence-based observation from leadership/governance pages, impact materials, public reports, partner proof, recent updates, or donation-page trust signals.

Why it matters:
- Plain-language explanation tied to donor confidence, funder readiness, volunteer confidence, partner trust, or public credibility.

Recommended next step:
- One practical action that matches the nonprofit’s stated priority and 30-day capacity.

30-day action:
- Specific leadership, impact, transparency, partner proof, update, or trust-signal improvement.

90-day opportunity:
- Larger transparency hub, impact summary, or public trust-building process if the team has capacity.

Evidence used:
- Source URL or intake field.

Needs confirmation:
- Any unclear or unverified item.
```

Tone rules:

- Warm, practical, and respectful.
- Avoid sounding like a compliance audit.
- Focus on public trust and ease of discovery.
- Do not imply wrongdoing.
- Do not overstate certainty.

## 13. Example Output Snippets

### Strong Example

```text
What we saw: The website includes a leadership page, recent program updates, and an impact page linked from the main navigation.

Why it matters: These materials help new donors, volunteers, funders, and partners understand who is behind the work and what the organization is currently accomplishing.

Recommended next step: Add one short transparency section near the donation path that links to the impact page and leadership page.
```

### Missing Evidence Example

```text
What we saw: We could not find public leadership or board information from the About page, footer, or main navigation.

Why it matters: Basic leadership visibility can help outside audiences understand accountability and feel more confident engaging with the organization.

Recommended next step: Add a simple public leadership or board page with appropriate names and roles, or link to an existing public profile if that is the safer option.
```

### Needs Confirmation Example

```text
What we saw: The intake notes mention an annual report, but we could not confirm a public link to it on the website.

Why it matters: If the report is intended to be public, making it easier to find could strengthen donor and funder confidence.

Recommended next step: Confirm whether the annual report should be public, then add a link or create a plain-language public summary if appropriate.
```
