# Expert Knowledge — SEO / GEO

**Owner:** connectNPO operations  
**Status:** Draft v1  
**Use:** Expert standard for the SEO / GEO section of the Growth Readiness Report.

This file helps the report agent evaluate whether a nonprofit website is understandable and discoverable through search engines and AI answer tools. GEO means Generative Engine Optimization: making public content clear enough for AI systems to understand, summarize, and cite accurately.

## 2. What This Area Evaluates

SEO / GEO evaluates whether the nonprofit’s public website uses clear, searchable, answer-friendly language.

A strong SEO / GEO foundation should make these things clear:

- What services, programs, or resources the nonprofit provides.
- Who the nonprofit serves.
- Where the nonprofit works, if location or service area matters.
- What questions the organization can credibly answer.
- Which pages contain authoritative information about mission, programs, impact, and ways to get involved.
- Whether page titles, headings, and content use words that real people would search or ask an AI tool.

This area does not promise rankings, traffic growth, AI citations, or fundraising outcomes. It focuses on public clarity, structure, and discoverability readiness.

## 3. Intake Signals to Use

Relevant intake fields:

```text
- Field/key: search_importance
  Why it matters: Shows whether discovery through Google or AI answers is a current priority.
  Strong signal: “Very important” or “we want people to find services we provide.”
  Weak or missing signal: Search visibility is not a current priority or priority is unknown.

- Field/key: search_ai_discovery_concern
  Why it matters: Identifies the type of discoverability concern.
  Strong signal: Specific concern such as unclear titles, missing answers to common questions, or poor service discovery.
  Weak or missing signal: “We do not know” with no detail.

- Field/key: primary_goal
  Why it matters: Helps connect discoverability to donor, volunteer, service, funder, or partner needs.
  Strong signal: Clear goal that depends on people finding information online.
  Weak or missing signal: No connection between discovery and current objectives.

- Field/key: current_programs_or_services
  Why it matters: Search and AI tools need plain-language descriptions of programs and services.
  Strong signal: Programs are described in terms the public would use.
  Weak or missing signal: Internal program names without plain-language explanation.

- Field/key: service_area_or_community
  Why it matters: Location/service area affects search intent and AI answers for many nonprofits.
  Strong signal: Clear city, region, state, neighborhood, or audience scope where relevant.
  Weak or missing signal: Service area unclear or missing from public pages.
```

Rules:

- Do not claim ranking, traffic, or AI visibility problems without evidence.
- Do not promise ranking improvements.
- Use intake to understand goals and service language.
- Confirm public page structure and wording with website evidence.

## 4. Public Evidence to Collect

Primary evidence:

- Homepage title and meta description if available.
- Homepage H1/headline and major headings.
- Program/service page titles and headings.
- Whether services are described in plain searchable language.
- FAQ, question-answer, guide, resource, or explanatory content.
- About/Mission page clarity.
- Location or service-area language where relevant.
- Internal links between homepage, programs, impact, donate, volunteer, and contact pages.
- Basic schema/structured data only if easily observable.

For this area, collect:

```text
Evidence item: Page title and description clarity
Where to look: Browser title, metadata if available, search snippet if observable.
What counts as strong evidence: Title says organization name plus clear service/mission/location where appropriate.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Generic title like “Home” or organization name only with no useful context.
Suggested citation format: Homepage title/meta, [URL]

Evidence item: Heading and page structure
Where to look: Homepage and key program/service pages.
What counts as strong evidence: H1 and H2 headings explain services, audience, location, and next steps in plain language.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Headings are vague, internal, decorative, or missing.
Suggested citation format: Page heading review, [URL]

Evidence item: Searchable service language
Where to look: Program/service pages and homepage copy.
What counts as strong evidence: Content uses words the public would search, such as service type, audience, problem, and location.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Internal program names without explanation.
Suggested citation format: Program/service language, [URL]

Evidence item: Question-answer content
Where to look: FAQ, resource pages, guides, blog posts, service pages.
What counts as strong evidence: Site answers common audience questions directly and clearly.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: No FAQ or explanatory content; visitors must infer answers.
Suggested citation format: FAQ/resource content, [URL]

Evidence item: Service area clarity
Where to look: Homepage, About page, program pages, contact page, footer.
What counts as strong evidence: Public pages identify city, region, state, national scope, or online service scope where relevant.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Location or service area is missing or inconsistent.
Suggested citation format: Service-area language, [URL]
```

Evidence rules:

- Prefer public website evidence.
- Do not run intrusive crawls or heavy automated scans against nonprofit sites.
- Do not claim Google ranking positions unless directly checked and cited.
- Do not claim AI answer inclusion unless directly tested and documented as a snapshot.
- If metadata or schema is not easily observable, mark it `Not found` or `Not checked`, not as a failure.

## 5. Strong Signals

Strong SEO / GEO signals:

- Pages describe services using words the public would search or ask.
- Titles and headings are specific.
- The homepage clearly states who is served and what the organization does.
- Program pages explain services without relying only on internal names.
- The site answers common audience questions.
- Location or service area is clear where relevant.
- The site has authoritative pages for mission, programs, impact, ways to help, and contact.
- Internal links help visitors and crawlers understand page relationships.
- Content is factual and easy for AI tools to summarize without guessing.
- Trust and impact information are visible enough to support authoritative answers.

---

## 6. Warning Signs

SEO / GEO warning signs:

- Generic page titles such as “Home,” “Programs,” or organization name only.
- Internal program names without plain-language explanation.
- Homepage copy does not mention core services or audience.
- No FAQ, guide, resource, or question-answer content.
- Service area is unclear or inconsistent.
- Important pages are not linked clearly from the homepage or navigation.
- Pages use broad mission language but few concrete terms.
- Content is hard for a new visitor or AI tool to summarize accurately.
- Public pages do not answer the questions donors, volunteers, service users, partners, or funders are likely to ask.

Rules:

- Use supportive language.
- Avoid acting like a technical SEO audit unless technical evidence is available.
- Do not overstate the impact of metadata or schema.
- Phrase findings as discoverability and clarity opportunities.

## 7. Recommended Analysis Pattern

Use this structure:

```text
1. Start with the nonprofit’s stated search/AI discovery concern or priority.
2. Review titles, headings, service language, question-answer content, and service-area clarity.
3. Identify the main discoverability gap: unclear topic, unclear audience, unclear location, missing answers, or weak page structure.
4. Explain why the gap matters for people searching and for AI tools summarizing public information.
5. Recommend one practical 30-day improvement.
6. Recommend one 90-day content or structure improvement if capacity allows.
7. Mark missing or uncertain items as “Needs confirmation.”
```

Do not turn every SEO / GEO finding into a technical task. Start with content clarity, page structure, and answer-friendly wording.

## 8. Common Recommendations

### Recommendation 1: Improve homepage title and headline clarity

```text
When to use: Homepage title/headline is generic or does not explain the work.
Evidence required: Homepage title, meta, and hero/headline review.
30-day version: Update the title or headline to include organization name plus clear service/audience/location language where appropriate.
90-day version: Align titles and top-level headings across homepage, About, Programs, and Get Involved pages.
Effort level: Low
Expected benefit: Helps people and AI/search tools understand the organization faster.
Risk if ignored: Search snippets and AI summaries may lack useful context.
```

### Recommendation 2: Replace internal program names with plain explanations

```text
When to use: Program pages rely on branded/internal names without explaining services.
Evidence required: Program/service page review.
30-day version: Add one plain-language sentence under each program name explaining who it serves and what it provides.
90-day version: Build program pages around audience need, service description, eligibility/fit, and next steps.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Makes programs easier to find, understand, and summarize.
Risk if ignored: Visitors and AI tools may not know what the program actually does.
```

### Recommendation 3: Add FAQ or question-answer content

```text
When to use: The site does not answer common audience questions directly.
Evidence required: FAQ/resource/service page review + intake goals.
30-day version: Add 5–7 simple questions and answers on a relevant page.
90-day version: Create resource pages or guides that answer recurring donor, volunteer, service-user, or partner questions.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Improves usefulness for visitors and gives AI/search tools clearer answer material.
Risk if ignored: Visitors may need to contact staff for basic questions, or AI tools may summarize incomplete information.
```

### Recommendation 4: Clarify service area or community scope

```text
When to use: Location, region, or audience scope matters but is unclear.
Evidence required: Homepage, About, Programs, Contact, and footer review.
30-day version: Add a clear service-area sentence to the homepage, footer, or program pages.
90-day version: Create location/community-specific explanations if the nonprofit serves multiple areas or audiences.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Helps the right people understand whether the organization is relevant to them.
Risk if ignored: Searchers, partners, and AI tools may misunderstand geographic or community fit.
```

### Recommendation 5: Create clearer internal links between key pages

```text
When to use: Important pages exist but are not connected clearly.
Evidence required: Navigation, homepage, program, impact, donate/volunteer/contact page review.
30-day version: Add contextual links from homepage and program pages to the most important next-step pages.
90-day version: Build topic clusters around programs, impact, ways to help, and common questions.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Helps visitors and crawlers understand relationships between pages.
Risk if ignored: Useful content may remain hard to discover.
```

### Recommendation 6: Add concise impact and trust context to searchable pages

```text
When to use: Pages describe services but provide little authority, proof, or current impact context.
Evidence required: Program page, impact page, trust/transparency page review.
30-day version: Add one short impact/trust note to key service pages, such as an outcome example, community proof, or link to impact information.
90-day version: Create stronger public evidence pages that connect programs, impact, transparency, and ways to help.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Supports better visitor confidence and more accurate AI/search summaries.
Risk if ignored: Public content may be technically findable but not persuasive or authoritative.
```

## 9. What NOT to Say

Do not say:

- “This will improve your Google ranking.”
- “AI tools will cite your website after this.”
- “Your SEO is bad.”
- “Google is penalizing your site.”
- “You need schema markup” unless the evidence and use case justify it.
- “You need to publish a blog every week.”
- “This page has no value.”
- “Search visibility is the main problem” if intake says it is not a priority.

Safer alternatives:

- “This may make the page easier for people and search/AI tools to understand.”
- “Clearer titles and headings can improve discoverability readiness.”
- “Adding question-answer content may help the site answer common audience questions more directly.”
- “This is a content and discoverability readiness observation, not a ranking guarantee.”
- “Search visibility should be prioritized only if it supports the organization’s current goals.”

## 13. Example Output Snippets

### Strong Example

```text
What we saw: The homepage headline and program page headings use plain language to explain who the organization serves and what support is available.

Why it matters: This helps both visitors and search/AI tools understand when the organization is relevant to a specific need.

Recommended next step: Add a short FAQ section answering the most common questions from donors, volunteers, or people seeking services.
```

### Missing Evidence Example

```text
What we saw: We could not find public FAQ or question-answer content that explains common service or participation questions.

Why it matters: Without direct answers, visitors may need to contact staff for basic information, and AI tools may have less reliable public material to summarize.

Recommended next step: Add 5–7 simple questions and answers to the most relevant program or Get Involved page.
```

### Needs Confirmation Example

```text
What we saw: The intake notes mention a specific service area, but the public website does not clearly state the city, region, or community scope.

Why it matters: People searching for local support may not know whether the organization serves them, and search/AI tools may summarize the service area incorrectly.

Recommended next step: Confirm the current service area, then add a clear service-area sentence to the homepage and key program pages.
```
