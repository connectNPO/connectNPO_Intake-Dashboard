## 2. What This Area Evaluates

Volunteer Readiness evaluates whether a nonprofit makes it easy for potential volunteers to understand where they fit, what is expected, and how to take the next step.

A strong volunteer path should make these things clear:

- Whether the organization currently uses volunteers.
- What roles or types of help are needed.
- Who the roles are best suited for.
- Time commitment, location, schedule, or flexibility.
- Training, onboarding, background check, or eligibility expectations if relevant.
- How to apply, sign up, ask a question, or express interest.
- What happens after someone submits interest.

This area does not evaluate HR compliance, screening legality, background-check law, or volunteer management policy beyond public-facing clarity and operational readiness.

## 3. Intake Signals to Use

Relevant intake fields:

```text
- Field/key: volunteer_situation
  Why it matters: Shows whether volunteer recruitment is a priority and what feels stuck.
  Strong signal: Specific issue such as “roles are not clearly explained” or “people do not know the next step.”
  Weak or missing signal: General “we need more volunteers” without role or process detail.

- Field/key: primary_goal
  Why it matters: Helps decide whether volunteer readiness should be prioritized in the report.
  Strong signal: Clear goal around recruiting, onboarding, or organizing volunteers.
  Weak or missing signal: Volunteer growth is not connected to current priorities.

- Field/key: current_programs_or_services
  Why it matters: Volunteer roles should connect to real program needs.
  Strong signal: Programs have clear support needs or public participation opportunities.
  Weak or missing signal: Volunteer need is stated but not tied to programs.

- Field/key: implementation_capacity_30_days
  Why it matters: Keeps recommendations realistic.
  Strong signal: Capacity for small copy edits, role descriptions, or a simple interest form.
  Weak or missing signal: Capacity unknown.

- Field/key: current_tools_or_workflows
  Why it matters: Volunteer intake may connect to simple automation opportunities.
  Strong signal: Existing form, spreadsheet, email process, or CRM is described.
  Weak or missing signal: No clear way to track volunteer interest.
```

Rules:

- Do not assume the nonprofit should recruit volunteers if intake says it is not a current priority.
- Do not request volunteer personal data, background check information, or private HR records.
- Use intake to understand roles and capacity.
- Confirm public-facing volunteer path with website evidence when possible.

## 4. Public Evidence to Collect

Primary evidence:

- Volunteer page or section.
- Homepage volunteer CTA if present.
- Ways to Help / Get Involved page.
- Role descriptions.
- Time commitment or schedule language.
- Location or remote/virtual opportunity language.
- Signup form, interest form, email, or contact path.
- Training, onboarding, or expectations language if public.
- Program pages that mention volunteer involvement.

For this area, collect:

```text
Evidence item: Volunteer CTA visibility
Where to look: Homepage, header, footer, Get Involved page.
What counts as strong evidence: Volunteer action is easy to find and clearly labeled.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Volunteer action is hidden, vague, or only mentioned casually.
Suggested citation format: Volunteer CTA, [URL]

Evidence item: Role clarity
Where to look: Volunteer page, Get Involved page, program pages.
What counts as strong evidence: Roles are named in plain language with basic duties or examples.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Page asks people to volunteer without explaining possible roles.
Suggested citation format: Volunteer role section, [URL]

Evidence item: Time commitment and expectations
Where to look: Volunteer page, application page, FAQ, onboarding information.
What counts as strong evidence: Basic time, location, training, or expectation details are visible.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Interested people must apply or email before knowing basic expectations.
Suggested citation format: Volunteer expectations, [URL]

Evidence item: Signup or contact path
Where to look: Volunteer page, contact page, forms.
What counts as strong evidence: Clear next step, form, email, or contact path exists.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: No clear path or a generic contact page with no volunteer context.
Suggested citation format: Volunteer signup/contact path, [URL]

Evidence item: Onboarding or follow-up expectation
Where to look: Volunteer page, confirmation text, FAQ, application instructions.
What counts as strong evidence: Visitor knows what happens after submitting interest.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: No explanation of review, response time, training, or next step.
Suggested citation format: Volunteer onboarding language, [URL]
```

Evidence rules:

- Prefer the nonprofit’s own public website first.
- Do not submit volunteer forms with fake or real personal information.
- Do not request private volunteer lists, applications, screening records, or background-check data.
- If no volunteer page is found, label it `Not found`; do not assume the organization does not use volunteers.

## 5. Strong Signals

Strong Volunteer Readiness signals:

- Volunteer action is visible from the homepage or Get Involved path.
- Roles are described in plain language.
- Time commitment, schedule, location, or flexibility is explained.
- The signup/contact path is clear.
- The page explains what happens after someone expresses interest.
- Expectations, training, or onboarding are described at a basic level.
- Volunteer opportunities connect to real programs or impact.
- The tone is welcoming and realistic.
- The nonprofit distinguishes one-time, ongoing, skilled, or group opportunities if relevant.
- The process appears manageable for the organization’s staff capacity.

---

## 6. Warning Signs

Volunteer Readiness warning signs:

- Volunteer CTA exists, but roles are vague.
- The website says “volunteer” without describing what volunteers do.
- Interested people must email before knowing basic expectations.
- No time commitment, location, or schedule information is visible.
- No onboarding or next-step expectation is explained.
- Volunteer request appears disconnected from current programs.
- The process may create staff burden because all inquiries go to a general inbox with no routing.
- Volunteer language is urgent but not specific.
- The organization says it needs volunteers, but the public site does not support recruitment.

Rules:

- Use supportive language.
- Avoid implying the nonprofit is unorganized or unsafe.
- Do not advise on legal screening or HR compliance.
- Phrase findings as clarity and process improvement opportunities.

## 7. Recommended Analysis Pattern

Use this structure:

```text
1. Start with the nonprofit’s stated volunteer situation or goal.
2. Review the public path from homepage or Get Involved page to volunteer action.
3. Identify the main readiness gap: visibility, role clarity, expectations, signup path, or onboarding.
4. Explain why the gap matters for interested volunteers and staff capacity.
5. Recommend one practical 30-day improvement.
6. Recommend one 90-day process or automation improvement if capacity allows.
7. Mark missing or uncertain items as “Needs confirmation.”
```

Do not recommend a complex volunteer management system unless the evidence shows enough volume or process complexity. Prefer clear roles, simple forms, and manageable follow-up first.

## 8. Common Recommendations

### Recommendation 1: Add or clarify a volunteer action path

```text
When to use: Volunteer CTA is missing, hidden, or unclear.
Evidence required: Homepage, footer, Get Involved, and volunteer page review.
30-day version: Add a clear Volunteer or Get Involved action that links to one simple next step.
90-day version: Build a dedicated volunteer page that explains roles, expectations, and follow-up process.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Helps interested supporters know how to take the next step.
Risk if ignored: People may want to help but leave without contacting the organization.
```

### Recommendation 2: Create simple volunteer role descriptions

```text
When to use: The site asks for volunteers but does not explain what roles exist.
Evidence required: Volunteer page and program page review.
30-day version: Add 3–5 common volunteer role examples with one-sentence descriptions.
90-day version: Build role cards with duties, time commitment, location, and skill level.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Helps people self-select into roles that match their interests and availability.
Risk if ignored: Staff may receive vague inquiries that require extra follow-up.
```

### Recommendation 3: Explain time commitment and expectations

```text
When to use: Volunteer role or signup path lacks basic expectations.
Evidence required: Volunteer page, signup form, FAQ, or onboarding language review.
30-day version: Add basic expectation notes such as typical time commitment, location, and whether training is provided.
90-day version: Create a volunteer onboarding overview with steps from interest to first activity.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Reduces uncertainty for new volunteers and helps staff answer fewer repeated questions.
Risk if ignored: Interested people may hesitate because they do not know what they are committing to.
```

### Recommendation 4: Improve the volunteer interest form or contact path

```text
When to use: The only next step is a generic email/contact page, or there is no clear routing.
Evidence required: Contact path and form review.
30-day version: Create a short volunteer interest form or add volunteer-specific instructions to the contact page.
90-day version: Route volunteer inquiries into a simple tracking workflow, spreadsheet, CRM, or shared inbox process.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Makes follow-up easier and helps staff understand volunteer interest at a glance.
Risk if ignored: Inquiries may be missed, delayed, or hard to organize.
```

### Recommendation 5: Add “what happens next” language

```text
When to use: The site has a form or email path but does not explain response time, review, training, or next step.
Evidence required: Volunteer page and form confirmation/review if visible.
30-day version: Add a short paragraph explaining what happens after someone submits interest.
90-day version: Create a basic welcome email or volunteer onboarding sequence.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Sets expectations and makes the process feel more trustworthy.
Risk if ignored: Volunteers may submit interest but feel unsure whether anything happened.
```

### Recommendation 6: Connect volunteer roles to mission impact

```text
When to use: Volunteer page lists tasks but does not explain why they matter.
Evidence required: Volunteer page, program page, and impact language review.
30-day version: Add a short impact statement under each role or next to the volunteer CTA.
90-day version: Add volunteer stories or examples that show how volunteers support outcomes.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Helps volunteers see their contribution as meaningful, not just administrative.
Risk if ignored: Roles may feel less motivating even when the work is important.
```

## 9. What NOT to Say

Do not say:

- “Your volunteer program is disorganized.”
- “Your screening process is inadequate.”
- “You are not compliant with volunteer laws.”
- “You should collect background-check information through this form.”
- “You need a volunteer management platform.”
- “This will increase volunteer signups by X%.”
- “Volunteers are not signing up because of this page.”

Safer alternatives:

- “The current volunteer path could make roles and next steps clearer for interested people.”
- “Adding basic expectations may reduce uncertainty before someone reaches out.”
- “A simple interest form may help staff organize inquiries more easily.”
- “Any screening, eligibility, or legal requirements should be reviewed by the appropriate advisor.”
- “This is an operational readiness observation, not a compliance determination.”

---

## 10. Safety, Privacy, and Disclaimer Rules

Use these rules:

- Do not request volunteer personal data, background-check information, ID documents, private applications, employee records, client/member data, or internal HR policies.
- Do not provide legal, HR, screening, insurance, tax, accounting, or compliance advice.
- Do not promise volunteer recruitment results.
- Do not infer volunteer volume, retention, or staff capacity beyond intake/public evidence.
- Distinguish public evidence from intake answers and assumptions.
- Label uncertain items as `Needs confirmation`.
- Draft for human review before customer delivery.

If Volunteer Readiness overlaps with **Financial & Compliance Readiness** or legal/HR concerns, keep the client-facing section operational only and include the appropriate human-review note. If a Financial & Compliance section is included, it must include:

> This section is an operational readiness review, not accounting, tax, or legal advice.

## 12. Report Output Guidance

Recommended client-facing section format:

```text
### Volunteer Readiness

What we saw:
- Evidence-based observation from the volunteer page, CTA, role descriptions, expectations, signup path, or onboarding language.

Why it matters:
- Plain-language explanation tied to volunteer confidence, staff capacity, or program support.

Recommended next step:
- One practical action that matches the nonprofit’s stated volunteer goal and 30-day capacity.

30-day action:
- Specific role, CTA, form, expectation, or onboarding language improvement.

90-day opportunity:
- Larger volunteer pathway, tracking, or onboarding improvement if the team has capacity.

Evidence used:
- Source URL or intake field.

Needs confirmation:
- Any unclear or unverified item.
```

Tone rules:

- Warm, practical, and respectful.
- Avoid sounding like a compliance or HR audit.
- Focus on clarity, confidence, and manageable follow-up.
- Do not shame the organization for limited staff capacity.
- Do not overstate certainty.

## 13. Example Output Snippets

### Strong Example

```text
What we saw: The website includes a visible Volunteer action and explains several role types with basic time commitment information.

Why it matters: This helps interested people quickly decide whether there is a role that fits their availability and interests.

Recommended next step: Add a short “what happens after you apply” note so volunteers know when and how the team will follow up.
```

### Missing Evidence Example

```text
What we saw: We could not find a public volunteer page or clear volunteer next step from the homepage or main navigation.

Why it matters: If volunteer recruitment is a priority, interested supporters may not know whether help is needed or how to get involved.

Recommended next step: Add a simple Get Involved or Volunteer section with 3–5 role examples and a clear contact or interest form.
```

### Needs Confirmation Example

```text
What we saw: The intake notes mention that volunteers help with events, but we could not confirm public role descriptions or time expectations on the website.

Why it matters: Event volunteers may be more likely to respond when they can understand the type of help needed and the expected commitment.

Recommended next step: Confirm the current event volunteer roles, then add a short role list and next-step instructions to the volunteer page.
```
