## 2. What This Area Evaluates

Donor Conversion evaluates whether the nonprofit makes it easy for a motivated supporter to understand the need, trust the organization, and take the next giving-related action.

A strong donor path should make these things clear:

- Why donations matter now.
- What gifts help make possible.
- How to donate or support the work.
- Whether the giving path feels trustworthy.
- What happens after someone gives.
- Whether giving language matches the nonprofit’s current goals and capacity.

This area does **not** evaluate accounting, tax compliance, fundraising legality, or donation platform security beyond observable public trust and usability signals.

---

## 3. Intake Signals to Use

Relevant intake fields:

```text
- Field/key: donor_challenge
  Why it matters: Shows the donor-related problem the nonprofit wants help with.
  Strong signal: Specific issue such as “we do not explain what donations make possible.”
  Weak or missing signal: “We need more donors” without context.

- Field/key: primary_goal
  Why it matters: Shows whether donor growth is the current priority.
  Strong signal: Clear fundraising, donor trust, or supporter engagement goal.
  Weak or missing signal: No clear connection between donor goal and website/report priority.

- Field/key: stuck_points
  Why it matters: Connects donor conversion to a practical blocker.
  Strong signal: “People care about our mission but do not donate” or “donation page is too generic.”
  Weak or missing signal: No stated donor friction.

- Field/key: current_donor_communication
  Why it matters: Shows whether the organization already updates or follows up with donors.
  Strong signal: Regular impact updates, donor newsletters, or thank-you process.
  Weak or missing signal: No clear follow-up or impact update path.

- Field/key: implementation_capacity_30_days
  Why it matters: Keeps donor recommendations realistic.
  Strong signal: Capacity for copy edits, donation page updates, or a small campaign page.
  Weak or missing signal: Capacity unknown.
```

Rules:

- Do not treat intake answers as proof that donors behave a certain way.
- Do not infer conversion rates, donation volume, donor retention, or revenue.
- Use intake to understand goals and friction.
- Confirm donation path and gift messaging with public evidence when possible.

## 4. Public Evidence to Collect

Primary evidence:

- Homepage donation/support CTA.
- Donate, Give, Support, or Ways to Help page.
- Gift impact language.
- Suggested giving amounts if public.
- Recurring giving option if public.
- Donor trust signals near the ask.
- Impact, annual report, testimonials, or outcome examples.
- Donation journey from homepage to donation action.
- Post-donation expectation language if visible.

For this area, collect:

```text
Evidence item: Donate CTA visibility
Where to look: Homepage header, hero section, footer, and key pages.
What counts as strong evidence: Donation/support action is easy to find and clearly labeled.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Donate action is hidden, generic, or only visible after searching.
Suggested citation format: Homepage CTA, [URL]

Evidence item: Gift impact language
Where to look: Donate page, homepage, campaign pages, impact pages.
What counts as strong evidence: Gifts are connected to concrete outcomes, services, or community benefit.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Generic “support us” language without explaining what giving helps do.
Suggested citation format: Donate page gift impact section, [URL]

Evidence item: Donor trust signals near the ask
Where to look: Donate page, footer, impact page, annual report, transparency page.
What counts as strong evidence: Public impact proof, financial transparency link, leadership/board info, secure donation platform, or clear contact path near giving decision.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Donation request appears without context, impact proof, or trust support.
Suggested citation format: Donate page trust signal, [URL]

Evidence item: Donor journey clarity
Where to look: Homepage to donate page path.
What counts as strong evidence: Visitor can move from mission understanding to giving action with minimal confusion.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Multiple unclear actions, broken links, buried donation page, or confusing external platform handoff.
Suggested citation format: Homepage-to-donate path, [URL]

Evidence item: Follow-up or donor relationship language
Where to look: Donate page, newsletter signup, thank-you/process language if public.
What counts as strong evidence: Donors know they will receive updates, impact stories, or next-step communication.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: No visible expectation of what happens after giving.
Suggested citation format: Donor follow-up language, [URL]
```

Evidence rules:

- Prefer public website evidence.
- Do not test donations with real payment information.
- Do not ask for donor lists, donation platform access, payment processor access, or private fundraising reports.
- If donation details are hosted on a third-party platform, cite only publicly visible pages.
- If no donation page is found, label it `Not found`; do not assume the organization cannot receive donations.

## 5. Strong Signals

Strong Donor Conversion signals:

- Donation action is visible from the homepage.
- Donate/support wording is clear and easy to understand.
- Donation page explains what gifts help accomplish.
- Suggested amounts are connected to practical impact when appropriate.
- Recurring giving is visible if the nonprofit offers it.
- Donor trust signals appear near the giving request.
- Impact stories, outcomes, or examples support the ask.
- The donation journey has few steps and little confusion.
- Contact information is available for donor questions.
- Messaging makes donors feel like partners in the mission, not just payment sources.

---

## 6. Warning Signs

Donor Conversion warning signs:

- Donate action is hard to find.
- The donation page uses generic “support us” language only.
- The page asks for money before explaining the need or impact.
- Gift amounts are listed without explaining what they help make possible.
- Trust or transparency information is far away from the ask.
- Donation path sends visitors to an external platform with little context.
- There is no clear follow-up expectation after giving.
- Multiple donation/support CTAs compete with no clear priority.
- Donor language focuses on the organization’s needs but not the community outcome.
- The nonprofit says donor growth is important, but the public website does not support that goal clearly.

Rules:

- Use supportive language.
- Avoid saying donor behavior is caused by one website issue.
- Avoid promising fundraising results.
- Phrase issues as donor-path improvement opportunities.

## 7. Recommended Analysis Pattern

Use this structure:

```text
1. Start with the nonprofit’s stated donor-related goal or challenge.
2. Review the public path from homepage to donation/support action.
3. Identify the strongest donor-path gap: visibility, motivation, trust, impact, or follow-up.
4. Explain why the gap matters for interested supporters.
5. Recommend one practical 30-day improvement.
6. Recommend one 90-day donor journey improvement if capacity allows.
7. Mark missing or uncertain items as “Needs confirmation.”
```

Recommendations must connect to at least one of these:

- intake donor challenge,
- public donation path evidence,
- gift impact evidence,
- trust/transparency evidence,
- missing evidence that should be confirmed.

## 8. Common Recommendations

### Recommendation 1: Make the donation action easier to find

```text
When to use: Donate/support CTA is hidden, missing, or hard to recognize.
Evidence required: Homepage/header/footer review + donation page/path review.
30-day version: Add a clear Donate or Support Our Work action in the header, homepage, and footer.
90-day version: Build a simple donor pathway from homepage to donation page to impact follow-up.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Helps motivated visitors take action without searching.
Risk if ignored: Visitors may support the mission but leave before finding the giving path.
```

### Recommendation 2: Add gift impact examples

```text
When to use: Donation page asks for support but does not explain what giving makes possible.
Evidence required: Donation page and impact/program page review.
30-day version: Add 3 short examples showing what different gift levels or types of support help accomplish.
90-day version: Create an impact-based giving page or campaign page tied to current programs.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Makes the ask more concrete and meaningful.
Risk if ignored: Donors may understand the mission but not the value of giving now.
```

### Recommendation 3: Place trust signals near the donation ask

```text
When to use: Donation request appears without nearby impact, transparency, leadership, contact, or credibility signals.
Evidence required: Donation page + transparency/impact page review.
30-day version: Add nearby links or short proof points such as impact summary, annual report, leadership page, or contact information.
90-day version: Build a donor trust block that combines impact, transparency, and accountability links.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Helps supporters feel more confident before giving.
Risk if ignored: Interested donors may hesitate because they cannot quickly confirm credibility.
```

### Recommendation 4: Clarify the donation page message

```text
When to use: Donation page language is generic or organization-centered.
Evidence required: Donation page copy review.
30-day version: Rewrite the opening paragraph to explain the need, the community benefit, and why giving matters now.
90-day version: Align donor messaging across homepage, donation page, email signup, and campaign content.
Effort level: Low
Expected benefit: Makes the donation request easier to understand and emotionally connect with.
Risk if ignored: The ask may feel disconnected from mission impact.
```

### Recommendation 5: Add a donor follow-up expectation

```text
When to use: Public giving path does not say whether donors will receive updates, receipts, impact stories, or next steps.
Evidence required: Donation page, newsletter, and donor communication review.
30-day version: Add a short line explaining how supporters can stay updated after giving.
90-day version: Create a simple donor welcome/update sequence or recurring impact email plan.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Helps donors feel like continuing partners in the mission.
Risk if ignored: Donors may give once but not understand how they remain connected.
```

### Recommendation 6: Reduce friction in the homepage-to-donate journey

```text
When to use: The path from homepage to donation action has too many unclear steps, confusing labels, or awkward external handoffs.
Evidence required: Homepage-to-donate path review.
30-day version: Add short context before the external donation link so visitors know where they are going and why.
90-day version: Create a dedicated giving landing page that explains impact, trust, gift options, and the external checkout path.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Makes the giving process feel clearer and safer.
Risk if ignored: Visitors may abandon the path when it feels confusing or abrupt.
```

## 9. What NOT to Say

Do not say:

- “This is why people are not donating.”
- “This will increase donations by X%.”
- “Donors do not trust you.”
- “Your fundraising strategy is wrong.”
- “You are not compliant.”
- “Your donation platform is unsafe” unless verified by a clear public security issue and human review.
- “You should change tax language, receipting, or charitable solicitation practices.”

Safer alternatives:

- “This may make the giving path clearer for interested supporters.”
- “Adding gift impact examples can help donors understand what their support makes possible.”
- “Trust signals near the ask may reduce uncertainty for first-time donors.”
- “This item should be reviewed by the appropriate legal, tax, or accounting advisor if it affects compliance or receipting.”
- “This is an operational readiness observation, not a fundraising outcome guarantee.”

## 10. Safety, Privacy, and Disclaimer Rules

Use these rules:

- Do not request donor lists, donor names, payment information, fundraising reports, CRM access, analytics access, donation platform access, passwords, or financial statements.
- Do not provide legal, tax, accounting, charitable solicitation, receipting, or compliance advice.
- Do not promise fundraising results.
- Do not infer donor demographics, donor intent, conversion rates, or revenue.
- Distinguish public evidence from intake answers and assumptions.
- Label uncertain items as `Needs confirmation`.
- Draft for human review before customer delivery.

If Donor Conversion overlaps with **Financial & Compliance Readiness**, the related client-facing section must include:

> This section is an operational readiness review, not accounting, tax, or legal advice.

## 12. Report Output Guidance

Recommended client-facing section format:

```text
### Donor Conversion

What we saw:
- Evidence-based observation from the homepage, donation path, gift impact language, trust signals, or follow-up language.

Why it matters:
- Plain-language explanation tied to donor understanding, confidence, and next action.

Recommended next step:
- One practical action that matches the nonprofit’s stated donor goal and 30-day capacity.

30-day action:
- Specific CTA, copy, gift impact, trust signal, or donor-path improvement.

90-day opportunity:
- Larger donor journey or follow-up improvement if the team has capacity.

Evidence used:
- Source URL or intake field.

Needs confirmation:
- Any unclear or unverified item.
```

Tone rules:

- Warm, practical, and respectful.
- Avoid sounding like a fundraising audit.
- Focus on clarity, confidence, and donor journey.
- Do not shame the organization for limited fundraising capacity.
- Do not overstate certainty.

## 13. Example Output Snippets

### Strong Example

```text
What we saw: The homepage includes a visible Donate action, and the donation page explains how gifts support current programs.

Why it matters: This gives interested supporters a clear path from mission interest to action and helps them understand what their gift can help make possible.

Recommended next step: Add one short impact example near the donation button so the giving request feels even more concrete.
```

### Missing Evidence Example

```text
What we saw: We could not find a public donation or support page from the homepage or main navigation.

Why it matters: If individual donor growth is a priority, interested visitors may not know how to contribute even if they support the mission.

Recommended next step: Add a visible Donate or Support Our Work path and include a short explanation of what donations help accomplish.
```

### Needs Confirmation Example

```text
What we saw: The intake notes mention recurring giving, but we could not confirm a recurring donation option on the public giving path.

Why it matters: If recurring giving is available, making that option easier to see could help supporters choose a more sustained way to help.

Recommended next step: Confirm whether recurring giving is currently available, then clarify the option on the donation page if appropriate.
```
