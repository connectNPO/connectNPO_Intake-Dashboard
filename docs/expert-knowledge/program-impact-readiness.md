## 2. What This Area Evaluates

Program & Impact Readiness evaluates whether the nonprofit has public-facing information and internal readiness signals that help outside audiences understand the work and take the next step.

A strong foundation should make these things clear:

- Program descriptions connect activities to outcomes.
- Impact proof is concrete.
- Stories and numbers support the same message.
- Programs explain who is served and what changes.
- Outputs and outcomes are not confused.
- Impact information is easy to find.

This area is an operational readiness review only. It should help connect intake answers, public evidence, expert standards, and practical next steps without overclaiming.

## 3. Intake Signals to Use

Relevant intake fields:

```text
- Field/key: impact_description
  Why it matters: Shows how the nonprofit currently describes impact.
  Strong signal: Clear outcome data, stories, outputs, feedback, or examples.
  Weak or missing signal: Impact is known internally but not written clearly.

- Field/key: proof_available_now
  Why it matters: Identifies realistic proof that can be used without overclaiming.
  Strong signal: Stories, numbers served, feedback, completion data, or partner quotes.
  Weak or missing signal: No proof source identified yet.

- Field/key: current_programs_or_services
  Why it matters: Impact analysis must connect to specific programs.
  Strong signal: Programs have clear audience and activities.
  Weak or missing signal: Programs are vague or outdated.

- Field/key: grant_related_materials
  Why it matters: Impact language also supports grant readiness.
  Strong signal: Outcomes/evaluation materials exist.
  Weak or missing signal: Grant materials lack measurable language.

- Field/key: implementation_capacity_30_days
  Why it matters: Keeps recommendations realistic.
  Strong signal: Capacity for a small proof block, impact story, or numbers update.
  Weak or missing signal: Capacity unknown.
```

Rules:

- Do not treat intake answers as proven public facts.
- Use intake to understand goals, constraints, and stated concerns.
- Confirm outward-facing claims with public evidence when possible.
- If a signal is missing or private, mark it as `Needs confirmation`.

## 4. Public Evidence to Collect

Primary evidence:

- Program pages.
- Impact page.
- Annual report.
- Stories/testimonials.
- Metrics or outcomes language.

For this area, collect:

```text
Evidence item: Program pages
Where to look: Programs/services/work pages.
What counts as strong evidence: Programs explain audience, need, activities, and next step.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Programs only list names or activities.
Suggested citation format: Program page, [URL]

Evidence item: Impact page
Where to look: Impact/results/outcomes pages.
What counts as strong evidence: Concrete results, stories, numbers, or learning are shown.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Impact is broad or missing.
Suggested citation format: Impact page, [URL]

Evidence item: Annual report
Where to look: Annual/impact report links.
What counts as strong evidence: Public report includes program results or examples.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Report missing, outdated, or hard to find.
Suggested citation format: Annual/impact report, [URL]

Evidence item: Stories/testimonials
Where to look: News, stories, testimonials, program pages.
What counts as strong evidence: Stories support the program impact message.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Stories are absent or disconnected.
Suggested citation format: Story/testimonial evidence, [URL]

Evidence item: Metrics or outcomes language
Where to look: Program, impact, report, grant-facing pages.
What counts as strong evidence: Outputs and outcomes are clearly distinguished.
What counts as weak/missing evidence: Activities are described without evidence of results.
Suggested citation format: Outcome/metric language, [URL]
```

Evidence rules:

- Prefer public materials linked from the nonprofit's own website.
- Do not request or store sensitive/private documents.
- If evidence is not visible publicly, label it `Not found` or `Needs confirmation`.
- Do not infer private facts from public silence.

## 5. Strong Signals

- Program descriptions connect activities to outcomes.
- Impact proof is concrete.
- Stories and numbers support the same message.
- Programs explain who is served and what changes.
- Outputs and outcomes are not confused.
- Impact information is easy to find.
- Proof is current enough for outside audiences.
- Claims are appropriately limited and not exaggerated.

---

## 6. Warning Signs

- Only activities are described.
- Outcomes are vague.
- Impact information is missing or hard to find.
- Stories exist but do not connect to program goals.
- Numbers are listed without context.
- Aspirations are presented like proven outcomes.
- Public program pages do not match intake descriptions.

Rules:

- Use supportive language.
- Avoid blame or audit-like phrasing.
- Avoid legal, tax, accounting, HR, or compliance determinations.
- Phrase findings as readiness improvement opportunities.

## 7. Recommended Analysis Pattern

Use this structure:

```text
1. Start with the nonprofit's stated concern or goal.
2. Compare intake signals with public evidence.
3. Identify the main readiness gap.
4. Explain why the gap matters for donors, volunteers, funders, partners, staff, or community members.
5. Recommend one practical 30-day improvement.
6. Recommend one 90-day improvement if capacity allows.
7. Mark uncertain items as “Needs confirmation.”
```

## 8. Common Recommendations

### Recommendation 1: Add a program impact block

```text
When to use: Program pages describe activities but not results.
Evidence required: Program and impact page review.
30-day version: Add one short block with audience, activity, and one proof point.
90-day version: Create a consistent impact structure across program pages.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Helps donors and funders understand program value.
Risk if ignored: Programs may sound active but not outcome-oriented.
```

### Recommendation 2: Use available proof without overclaiming

```text
When to use: The team has stories or numbers but limited outcome data.
Evidence required: Intake proof source + public pages.
30-day version: Add one story, output, or feedback example and label it clearly.
90-day version: Build a simple proof library with stories, outputs, feedback, and outcomes.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Improves credibility while staying accurate.
Risk if ignored: Impact claims may remain too broad.
```

### Recommendation 3: Separate outputs from outcomes

```text
When to use: Activities, participation numbers, and outcomes are mixed together.
Evidence required: Impact/report/program language review.
30-day version: Edit copy to distinguish what was done from what changed.
90-day version: Create a basic measurement narrative for key programs.
Effort level: Medium
Expected benefit: Makes reporting clearer for funders and partners.
Risk if ignored: Readers may not know what the organization actually achieved.
```

### Recommendation 4: Add current impact examples

```text
When to use: Impact proof appears outdated or buried.
Evidence required: News, annual report, impact page review.
30-day version: Add a recent example or update to the impact/program page.
90-day version: Create a quarterly impact update rhythm.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Shows current activity and momentum.
Risk if ignored: Public pages may feel stale.
```

### Recommendation 5: Connect impact to next action

```text
When to use: Impact is described but not tied to donate, volunteer, partner, or contact paths.
Evidence required: Impact page + CTA review.
30-day version: Add a relevant next step after the impact example.
90-day version: Build audience-specific pathways from impact proof to action.
Effort level: Low to Medium
Expected benefit: Helps motivated visitors respond.
Risk if ignored: Impact may inform visitors but not move them to action.
```

## 9. What NOT to Say

Do not say:

- “Your impact is unproven.”
- “This outcome is statistically valid.”
- “Funders will accept this evidence.”
- “You should publish client data.”
- “This proves your program works.”

Safer alternatives:

- “Public impact proof could be clearer.”
- “This evidence should be labeled carefully before reuse.”
- “A simple story or output can be useful when outcome data is limited.”

---

## 10. Safety, Privacy, and Disclaimer Rules

Use these rules:

- Do not request passwords, donor lists, employee records, private client/member data, bank information, private financial statements, confidential grant files, or sensitive internal documents.
- Do not provide legal, tax, accounting, HR, insurance, grant eligibility, or compliance advice.
- Do not promise fundraising, ranking, grant, volunteer, or operational outcomes.
- Distinguish public evidence from intake answers and assumptions.
- Label uncertain items as `Needs confirmation`.
- Draft for human review before customer delivery.
- Keep recommendations operational and public-facing unless a later secure workflow is explicitly built.

If this area overlaps with **Financial & Compliance Readiness**, the related client-facing section must include:

> This section is an operational readiness review, not accounting, tax, or legal advice.

```text

- Evidence-based observation.

- Plain-language explanation tied to the nonprofit's stated priority.

Recommended next step:
- One practical action.

30-day action:
- Specific improvement that can be done with limited capacity.

- Any unclear or unverified item.
```

Tone rules:

- Avoid sounding like an audit.
- Be specific enough for the nonprofit to act.

## 13. Example Output Snippets

### Strong Example

```text
What we saw: Public materials clearly explain the main need, who is served, and what action a visitor can take next.

Why it matters: This helps outside audiences understand the organization without already knowing the internal context.

Recommended next step: Add one short supporting example or link near the main message so the claim is easier to verify.
```

### Missing Evidence Example

```text
What we saw: We could not find public evidence that explains this area clearly from the homepage or related pages.

Why it matters: If this area is a priority, visitors may not know how to understand or act on the information.

Recommended next step: Add a short plain-language section that explains the current status and next step.
```

### Needs Confirmation Example

```text
What we saw: The intake notes mention this material, but we could not confirm a public version on the website.

Why it matters: If the material is safe and intended to be public, making it easier to find could strengthen readiness.

Recommended next step: Confirm what can be shared publicly, then add a short summary or link.
```
